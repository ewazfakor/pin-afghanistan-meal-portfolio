## 1. Project Overview

From October 2022 to March 2023, I served as a **MEAL Assistant** under People in Need (PIN) Afghanistan's Winterization Cash Assistance project in Ghazni Province, a remote mountainous region predominantly inhabited by Hazara communities. Funded by the Afghanistan Humanitarian Fund (AHF), the project targeted **1,888 vulnerable households** across Malistan (75%) and Jaghatu (25%) districts, delivering unconditional multipurpose cash grants to cover heating fuel ($200), winter clothing ($74), and blankets ($57) based on household vulnerability tiers.

Working from PIN's Ghazni Field Office with regular field travel to Malistan, I supported the **full MEAL cycle** under the supervision of the MEAL Officer — from beneficiary verification and data quality assurance through post-distribution monitoring (PDM) and reporting to ReportHub and the donor.

## 3. Data Collection Tools

### 3.1 KoboToolbox/ODK — Post-Distribution Monitoring Form

I used a structured XLSForm deployed on KoboToolbox for PDM data collection. The form included modules on:

- **Cash utilization** (heating, clothing, blankets)
- **Market functionality** and price monitoring alignment
- **Satisfaction scoring** (1–5 Likert scale)
- **Protection and safety of access** (with female enumerator modules)
- **Unmet needs**

The form was **bilingual (Dari/English)** with skip logic and validation constraints to minimize entry errors.

### 3.2 Excel Beneficiary Verification & Tracking Sheet

I maintained a master Excel tracker linking beneficiary lists from the ESNA (Emergency Shelter and Non-Food Items) cluster vulnerability scoring to actual distributions. The tracker used:

- **Conditional formatting** to flag duplicates, missing CNIC numbers, and households scoring discrepancies between registration and verification
- **Weekly VLOOKUP checks** against the distribution master list to ensure 100% alignment

### 3.3 CFRM (Community Feedback and Response Mechanism) Log Sheet

I managed a standardized CFRM log capturing complaints, inquiries, and suggestions via suggestion boxes, phone hotlines, and direct verbal reports. Each entry was assigned:

- A **unique CFRM ID**
- **Categorization by type** (inclusion error, payment delay, amount dispute, protection concern)
- **Tracking through to resolution**, closed with beneficiary confirmation

## 4. Key Activities & My Contributions

### Beneficiary Data Entry & Verification
I entered and cross-verified registration data for **1,888 beneficiary households** into the project database, achieving **99.2% accuracy** against paper registration forms by implementing a **double-entry system** with Excel conditional formatting for outlier detection.

### Spot-Check Monitoring
I conducted **23 spot-check visits** across **12 villages** in Malistan District, randomly selecting 5% of beneficiaries per distribution round to verify identity, residence, and receipt of cash transfers. Spot-check reports were uploaded to the shared drive within **24 hours** of each visit.

### PDM Data Collection Support
I supported the PDM exercise by:
- Programming the Kobo form (XLSForm)
- Training **4 enumerators** on ODK Collect
- Directly supervising data collection for **189 sampled households** (10% with 95% confidence interval) across both districts within **3 weeks** of the distribution

### Data Quality Assurance (DQA)
I performed **weekly DQA checks** on KoboToolbox submissions, flagging:
- **14 records** with GPS coordinate deviations exceeding 100m
- **3 duplicate entries**
- **7 incomplete forms**

All issues were resolved within **48 hours** through enumerator callbacks.

### CFRM Awareness & Documentation
I printed and laminated **35 CFRM posters** in Dari, placed them at distribution points and community gathering areas, and logged **41 feedback entries** throughout the project cycle, achieving a **93% resolution rate** within the 5-day standard.

### Market Monitoring Data Entry
I entered weekly **Joint Market Monitoring Initiative (JMMI)** data from Malistan bazar into the FSAC (Food Security and Agriculture Cluster) template, tracking prices of heating fuel, children's clothing, and blankets to validate the cash transfer value adequacy.

### Monthly Reporting to ReportHub
I compiled and verified monthly **5W (Who, What, Where, When, Whom)** data for ReportHub submission, ensuring PIN's activities were accurately reflected in the OCHA-coordinated humanitarian response tracking system.

## 5. Challenges & Solutions

| **Challenge** | **Context** | **Solution Applied** |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Winter road closures** | Heavy snowfall blocked the Ghazni–Malistan road for 7–10 days at a time, preventing field travel during critical monitoring periods. | I shifted to phone-based spot-checks for 30% of sampled households using pre-collected phone numbers, documented call logs with timestamps, and submitted a remote monitoring protocol waiver approved by the MEAL Officer. |
| **Female beneficiary access** | Cultural norms in Hazara communities limited women's ability to speak directly with male enumerators or travel to distribution points. | I coordinated with the project team to recruit **2 female enumerators** from Malistan for PDM data collection, conducted female-only focus groups in home settings, and ensured **50% of the PDM sample** included female respondent representation. |
| **Data discrepancies in beneficiary lists** | Initial registration lists from community shuras contained 23 duplicate CNIC entries and 9 households with incorrect mobile money details. | I designed an Excel deduplication macro using CNIC + phone number concatenation, flagged discrepancies for the Project Manager to verify with shura leaders, and reduced payment rejection errors from **1.7% to 0.3%** before distribution. |

## 8. Lessons Learned

1. **Remote monitoring protocols must be pre-positioned.** Afghanistan's winter road closures are predictable — having phone numbers, consent for remote follow-up, and a pre-approved remote monitoring SOP before the snow season would have reduced monitoring gaps and enumerator anxiety about delayed reporting.

2. **Local enumerators are a quality and protection asset.** Recruiting female enumerators from Malistan itself not only improved data quality through cultural access but also built trust with communities. In future projects, I would advocate for budgeting local enumerator recruitment from the design phase rather than treating it as an operational afterthought.

3. **Excel + Kobo integration is underutilized at the assistant level.** Learning intermediate Excel skills (VLOOKUP, INDEX-MATCH, conditional formatting, data validation) enabled me to perform DQA checks that would typically require specialized software. Investing time in these skills early accelerated my contribution to the team and reduced dependency on the MEAL Officer for routine quality checks.
